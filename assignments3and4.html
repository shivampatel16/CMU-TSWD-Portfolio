Dataset Explanation üìù

  • The data for these visualizations is open source and describes the batting and bowling performances of the top cities in the 2019 ICC Cricket World Cup.
  • Though the circket World Cups are played among countries, these visualisations focus on a different aspect. They compare the performance of the cities in these countries which is in turn measured by the performance of the players that the city produced.
  • The intended audience for these visualizations (and the data) are people interested in cricket. This is because they heaviely contain technical jargon related to cricket which is not easily understandable by people who do not have a background in cricket.
  • The data points in the visualizations are batting average, bowling average, runs and wickets. These are cricket heavy and can be better understood from the detailed explanation given in this link. However, in simple terms, if you are batting, you try to score as many runs as possible and save your wickets. But if you are bowling, you try to take the wickets of the opponent team and make them score as less runs as possible. If you loose your wicket, you are 'out'.
  • Batting average is the ratio of the total number of runs scored by a player to the number of times the player has been out. Bowling average is the ratio of the numbers of runs conceded by a player to the number of wickets taken by the player. These averages are calculated on a player basis and are then averaged (for the players from a given city) to find the averages of the respective cities.
  • The source of these visualizations do not have a built in data set (in tabular form). However, the data set was easily reconstructable based on the values given in the visualizations. Thus, I (easily) recreated the data set for building my sketches and the final visualizations in Flourish.

Step 2: Critique the data visualization


As a part of the assignment, I was required to critique the original visualization using Stephen Few's Data Visualization Effectiveness Profile. Stephen proposed seven criteria for evaluating the effectiveness of a data visualization. These seven criteria are divided into two broad categories, namely 'Informative' (Usefulness, Completeness, Perceptibility, Truthfulness, Intuitiveness) and 'Emotive' .

The metrics under Informative helps measure the effectiveness of the visualization in producing understanding to the audience. And the metrics under Emotive helps measure the effectiveness of the visualization in producing useful emotional response to the audience. I critique the original visualizations below.

Informative üìä

1. Usefulness

Is it useful for the intended audience? Does it communicate valuable information?


  • The visualizations are useful to the intended audience (cricket lovers), and they communicate relevant information (with additional efforts from the audience). However, they are not the best in conveying the relevant information.
  • At first site, considering the graphics in the visualizations, it seemed to me that the visualizations were trying to compare the sizes of the famous monuments across the cities!

2. Completeness

Does the visualization have everything necessary to make it understandable?


  • The visualizations are lacking in several aspects and thus are not complete to convey the entire information.
  • First of all, the title itself is incomplete. It does not state the year or the cricket tournament for which the visualisations were made.
  • Futher, they contain irrelevant information which adds to complexity and reduces their completeness. Our main focus is to compare the batting and bowling averages the best cities. Considering this, I do not find the details about runs ('Runs') and wickets ('Wkts') appropriate in this context.
  • Moreover, some of the sentences in the text are not required to convey the message that these visualizations aim for. Soem examples of such sentences include the following:

    "It is produced some of the most prolific batsman like David Warner, Steven Smith, Michael Clarke, Steve Waugh, and Mark Waugh."

    "The city has produced some very fine bowlers like Shane Bond, Richard Hadlee, Matt Henry and Chris Harris."

    "It is also the birthplace of England's hero from the final, Ben Stokes."
  • The large verbage in the visualizations details the message that these visualizations are trying to convey. It directly supports the fact that these visualizations lack clarity and thus are demanding further explanation by using text.

3. Perceptibility

Can the reader understand the information with minimal effort? Is the visualization type appropriate? Does it use illogical comparisons?


  • It took me a lot of effort to grasp the information that these visualizations are trying to convey.
  • There is a lot of unnecessary detail in the visualizations. The 3-4 line explanations are not needed. Moreover, the data about runs and wickets are not required in the respective graphs. Further, the titles of the visualizations are unclear and incomplete.
  • The chosen visualization type is not appropriate as well. Chosing the graphics of the famous buildings (in the cities) to represent the respective cities is not apt in this context. First of all, the users might not be aware of the buildings. Next, having buildings to represent the cities, adds a historical/monumental touch to the visualizations. As soon as I see famous monuments and values on top of them, I am motivated to think that these visualizations are comparing the heights of the famous monuments across the cities.
  • The buildings are of various sizes and placing the data points above these buildings brings an illogical comparison. It is human nature to think that taller objects should have more value than shorter objects. However, this does not hold true in this context. At multiple points we observe that taller objects (buildings) have lesser values (batting average, bowling average, runs, wickets) than shorter objects.

4. Truthfulness

Is the visualization accurate, reliable and valid? Is it representing what it says it is, and in the most complete and truthful manner? Does it misrepresent the data or make comparisions that aren't correct?


  • Being a cricket fan, I find the data to be accuracte; however, I do not find the visualization to be reliable. It seems attractive but can be misleading at first sight.
  • The different sized objects (buildings) used to represent the cities (having different bowling and batting averages!) highlight comparisons that are not correct and thus misrepresents the data.
  • The above insights, the incomplete titles, multiple irrelevant information, and the heavy use of dark pink and violet (in the background) colors, do not help in conveying the message in the most complete and truthful manner.

5. Intuitiveness

Is it easy to understand and clearly communicates the information? If unfamiliar, does it include easy to understand instructions on how to interpret it?


  • I do not find the visualizations easy to understand since they have used graphics (of various sizes) to represent something very simple. The graphics represent famous monuments in the respective cities; however, it would be difficult for the audience (even cricket lovers) to correctly identify the monuments. Even, I did not know some of them!
  • Such visualizations are not familiar to a large audience. I find the graphics to be distracting and irrelevant for the context and message to be conveyed. Moreover, there is no additional information provided to better interpret the visualizations.

Emotive üíù

6. Aesthetics

It is interesting/enjoyable to look at? Is it a good example of what a beautiful data visualization might look like? Is it somewhere in the middle - pleasing but otherwise not distracting to look at?


  • At first site, the visualization might come out to be attracitve, but later, it takes a lot of time to get value out of these visualizations.
  • I find the visualizations to be quite dull with the choice of colors. Moreover, there is huge amount of text in them.
  • The abbreviations used to highlight the data points (Eg: 'Wkts' and 'Avg') might not be known to a large audience. Having some knowledge about cricket and math, I could figure out that 'Wkts' is 'Wickets' and 'Avg' is 'Average'. But this might not hold true for all cricket lovers.
  • There is a lot of repeatation for each data point. The words 'Runs', 'Avg' and 'Wkts' are respectively repeated for each data point. They add to complexity and disturb the aesthetics of the visualizations. The repeatation clearly indicates that the message is not easily conveyed to the audience.
  • The background is filled with violet and it makes be feel sleepy üò¥üí§ by staring at it for long! Painting the whole background violet was definitely an incorrect choice.
  • The labels on the x-axis are colored in brown with a violet background. They hide properly in the background and unfortunately do not stand out well.

7. Engagement

Does it lead the audience to learn more about the topic? Does it inspire the audience to talk about the data or share it with others?


  • The first look at these visualizations and the fancy graphics of the monuments might catch ones attention but in line with the above discussion, they lack so much! The deficiencies (as discussed above) will not easily motivate the audience to learn more about the topic or dwell deeper into the data.
  • Being not a great visualization, it is not inspiring the audience to talk more about the data or share it with others. I would not be motivated to forward the link of the visualization to anybody.

Overall Observations

What stood out? üòÉ

  • The graphics of the famous monuments in the cities stood out at first site.
  • The violet background color caught my attention.
  • The dark pink color of the monuments grabbed my attention next.
  • Finally, the large amount of text made its way to my eyes, before I could start dwelling into what the visualizations had to say.

What worked well? ü§©

  • The large graphics of the monuments worked well in catching the first attention.
  • The large font size of titles were (somewhat) effective to convey what the visualizations were about.
  • The values for each data point. Though they are not properly represented, this worked out well because without them, I think the visualizations would become completely useless.

What did not work well? üòï

  • The background colored in violet.
  • Incomplete titles of the visualizations. They missed the year and tournament for which the data was displayed.
  • The huge amount of text (large sentences!) in the visualizations trying to convey the message.
  • Use of graphics of the famous monuments in the cities to respectively represent the cities.
  • Use of dark pink color in the graphics and in the background.
  • Repeatitions in the data points. 'Runs', 'Avg' and 'Wkts' were over and over again!
  • Use of abbreviations (for 'Avg' and 'Wkts') while representing the data points.
  • X-axis labels colored in light brown on top of a dull violet background.
  • The various heights of the graphical monuments.
  • The irrelevant details about 'Runs' and 'Wkts' in the respective visualizations.
  • Placing the data values of top of each graphical monument (of various heights).

What would you do differently? üìù

  • Remove the background color.
  • Update the titles of the visualisations.
  • Use a bar graph instead of graphical monuments to represent the data points.
  • Remove the large sentences from the visualizations.
  • Remove the details about 'Runs' and 'Wkts' from each data point.
  • Color the bars of the bar chart with relevant colors. I am planning to use the national cricket team colors of the respective countries the cities are a part of. It would perfectly align with the theme of Cricket World Cup.

How successful was this method at evaluating the data visualization you selected? Are there measures you feel are missing or not being captured here? What would you change? Provide 1-2 recommendations (color, type of visualization, layout, etc.).

  • I find the above evaluation and critiquing technique to be very effective of the data visualizations I selected. The evaluation method was detailed and helped evaluate various aspects of the visualizations.
  • I find this approach to be better than the Good Charts method. It is mainly because of the fact that this critiquing approach has lot more useful metrics to evaluate the visualizations than those used by the Good Charts method.
  • The Good Charts method guides one from the first sight of the visualization to suggesting what is to be changed in the visualization. The quantitative evaluation then follows for two metrics, namely 'Design Execution' and 'Contextual Awareness'.
  • I like Stephen Few's approach better because it has lot more quantitative evaluations which cover multiple aspects of the visualizations. Further, all the quantitative evaluations can be placed in one chart to get a better understanding of the visualizations being evaluated.

    • Recommendations:

  • As a recommendation, I would suggest adding 'Color' as an additional metric under the 'Emotive' category.
  • I understand that we are evaluating 'Aesthetics', but I believe that Aesthetics is a much broder umbrella as compared to Color. No doubt, we would evaluate Color when we are evaluating Aesthetics, but Color would be a small part of this evaluation.
  • Aesthetics would measure much more, including orientation, layout, visual appeal, color, choice of visualization, size, shape, etc.
  • Color on the other end, would evaluate a very specific and extremely important aspect of the visualizations. Such specific evaluation of a particular and important aspect of the visualizations could also help drive the focus during the redesign phase of the visualization.


Step 3: Sketch out a solution

To better convey the message of the original visualizations, I decided to incorporate the following changes in my initial sketches:

  • Type of Visualization: I changed the visualization type to bar charts and tree maps since they are much simpler and easy to understand as compared to the complex and disturbing graphical monuments in the original visualizations. However, upon comparing the bar charts and tree maps, I prefer going forward with bar charts, but I thought to decide upon this after testing them out with the interviewees.
  • Title: I changed the titles of the charts to make them more clear and effective in conveying the main takeaway to the audience.
  • Irrelevant Information: I removed the irrelevant information from the charts including the long sentences and the repeated text ('Runs', 'Wkts' and 'Avg') for each data point.
  • Color: This was an interesting decision! After quite some thought, I have colored the bars (in the bar chart) and areas (in the treemap) of the cities with their respective national cricket team colors.
  • Background Color: The background color in the original visualizations was not required and hence I have removed it in my sketches. It has made the sketches much more appealing.
  • Legend: Since I have colored the bars/areas based on the national cricket team colors of the cities, I thought it would be better to include a legend describing the colors of the countries. It would help the audience who is not well versed with the national cricket team colors of the cities.
After incorporating the above changes, I have sketched multiple wireframes for both the main (batting averages across the cities) and the bonus visualizations (bowling averages across the cities). Initially, I have sketched the visualizations by hand and then have converted a neater version of them using the Infogram wireframing tool.
